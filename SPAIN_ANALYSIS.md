# Spain ISDS Case Statistics

Analysis of Spain's 56 investor-state dispute settlement cases through 31 December 2023 (UNCTAD dataset). All monetary figures in USD millions unless stated.

> **Note on data coverage:** Amount data is available for 42 of 56 cases on the claims side and 28 of 56 on the awards side. Cases without amount data are excluded from financial calculations. Award figures reflect tribunal-issued amounts and do not account for subsequent annulments (e.g. Novenergia, where the award was later set aside by Swedish courts) or the Eiser resubmission award (Oct 2025, post-dataset).

---

Spain's 56 cases span 1997–2022, but 54 of the 56 (96%) were filed between 2011 and 2022 — driven by the solar and wind energy policy reversal under the Electricity Sector Law reforms.

**Total: 56 cases.** The 2015 spike reflects investors who filed after the full scope of Spain's retroactive subsidy cuts became clear.

---

## 2. Renewable energy share

**50 of 56 cases (89%)** fall in the electricity sector (ISIC Division 35 — "Electricity, gas, steam and air conditioning supply"). Virtually all of these concern solar photovoltaic, concentrated solar power (CSP), or wind energy investments that relied on Spain's renewable support regime.

The remaining 6 cases comprise: 2 financial services cases, 1 mining case, 1 manufacturing case, and 2 multi-sector cases (one of which includes electricity alongside other activities).

## 4. Concentration of claimant nationalities

The **top 5 home states** (Germany, Luxembourg, Netherlands, UK, Japan) account for **52 of 71 participations — 73%** of all claimant-country representations. The top 3 alone (Germany, Luxembourg, Netherlands) account for 61%.

This concentration reflects the structure of the European renewable energy fund industry: many funds are domiciled in Luxembourg or the Netherlands for tax and regulatory purposes, even when the ultimate investors are German or other EU nationals.

Awards are heavily compressed relative to claims: investors typically recover a fraction of what they sought, usually limited to actual damages under the FET standard rather than lost future profits.

## 9. Award-to-claim ratio

| Metric | Value |
|---|---|
| Aggregate (total awards ÷ total claims, matched cases) | 26.8% |
| Mean per-case ratio | 45.6% |
| Median per-case ratio | 49.2% |

The gap between the aggregate ratio (26.8%) and the per-case median (49.2%) reflects the outlier effect of the largest claims: PV Investors claimed USD 2,200M and was awarded USD 99.8M — a 4.5% ratio — which drags the aggregate down sharply. In a typical Spain ECT case, investors recovered roughly half of what they claimed.

Spain has notably settled none of its cases — an unusual stance for a state facing this volume of claims, and consistent with its broader non-compliance posture.

---

## 11. Spain's success rate in concluded disputes

Among the **37 cases with a decided outcome** (excluding the 15 pending and 4 discontinued):

- **Investor win rate: 75.7%** (28/37)
- **Spain win rate: 24.3%** (9/37)

This is one of the lowest state success rates of any repeatedly-sued country in the UNCTAD dataset. Spain's 9 wins are concentrated in cases where the investor failed to establish a legitimate expectation (typically early-stage or pre-operation investments) or where the claim was filed under a BIT rather than the ECT.

## 13. ECT share

**51 of 56 cases (91%)** were brought under the Energy Charter Treaty. Spain's ISDS exposure is almost entirely an ECT story.

---

## 14. Outcomes by treaty

| Treaty | Cases | Investor wins | State wins | Investor win rate (decided only) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| ECT | 51 | 27 | 6 | **82%** |
| BITs (all 5) | 5 | 1 | 3 | **25%** |

The stark divergence reflects the nature of the claims: ECT cases arise from the renewable energy subsidy reversal (a clear legitimate expectations violation under FET), whereas the BIT cases involve more varied factual situations where Spain's conduct was easier to defend. The one BIT investor win is Maffezini (1997), an early case predating the renewable wave.

## 16. Average duration of proceedings

Estimated from filing year to the year of the final decision in the UNCTAD decisions field (available for 43 cases):

| Metric | Value |
|---|---|
| Mean duration | 4.9 years |
| Median duration | 5.0 years |
| Range | 1–9 years |

ICSID proceedings before a full arbitral tribunal (the majority of Spain's cases) typically take 4–7 years from filing to award. Shorter durations tend to reflect jurisdictional dismissals; longer ones reflect complex damages phases or bifurcated proceedings.

---

## 17. Claim size and probability of success

Dividing decided cases with available claim data (n=36) into smaller and larger halves:

| Group | N | Investor win rate |
|---|---|---|
| Smaller claims (≤ USD 84M) | 18 | 72% |
| Larger claims (> USD 93M) | 18 | 78% |

There is **no meaningful relationship** between claim size and outcome. Investors win at roughly the same rate regardless of how large their claim is, consistent with FET claims being decided on the merits of the legitimate expectations analysis rather than on the quantum of loss.

## 18. Concentration of total liability

The **10 largest awards** account for **USD 1,138.7M of the USD 1,653.1M total — 68.9%** of Spain's total ICSID liability.

| Rank | Case | Award (USD M) |

| 2 | Eiser and Energía Solar v. Spain | 139.8 |
| 3 | Infrastructure Services / Antin v. Spain | 115.3 |
| 4 | Eurus Energy v. Spain | 109.9 |
| 5 | The PV Investors v. Spain | 99.8 |
| 6 | Watkins and others v. Spain | 85.3 |
| 7 | Masdar v. Spain | 76.7 |
| 8 | RREEF v. Spain | 66.0 |
| 9 | Novenergia v. Spain | 66.0 |
| 10 | Biram and others v. Spain | 56.3 |

**Notes:**
- Eiser's USD 139.8M reflects the original 2017 award (annulled 2020); the Oct 2025 resubmission award (~€262M, ~USD 285M) is not in the UNCTAD dataset and would move Eiser to second place.
- Novenergia's award (rank 9) was subsequently set aside by Swedish courts (Dec 2022/Jul 2023), reducing Spain's actual enforceable liability by USD 66M.
- NextEra's USD 323.6M award has been enforced by both the US DC Circuit (Aug 2024) and the Australian Federal Court (Aug 2025, appeal pending) — making it the single most consequential enforcement action against Spain to date.
