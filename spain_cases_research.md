# Spain Investment Disputes — Case Research Notes

**Compiled:** June 2026  
**Source dataset:** UNCTAD ISDS Navigator (through Dec 2023), supplemented with court decisions, legal commentary, and arbitration reporting through mid-2026.

## Background: The Spain ECT Wave

Spain's dispute-settlement exposure falls into two distinct categories:

**Pre-wave BIT cases (1997–2012):** A handful of claims under bilateral investment treaties (BITs), mostly involving non-energy sectors. Mostly resolved, some paid.

**Energy Charter Treaty (ECT) solar/wind wave (2012–2022):** Spain introduced generous feed-in tariff (FIT) regimes for photovoltaic, wind, and concentrated solar power (CSP) plants in 2007–2008 (Royal Decrees 661/07 and 1578/08). Facing fiscal pressure, Spain radically reformed these regimes between 2012 and 2014, retroactively cutting guaranteed tariffs and introducing a new "reasonable return" framework that yielded significantly lower revenues than investors had been promised. This triggered over 50 ECT arbitration claims, the largest wave of investor-state disputes ever filed against a single state.

**The intra-EU enforcement problem:** The ECJ's *Achmea* decision (2018) and *Komstroy* judgment (2021) held that ECT arbitration clauses are incompatible with EU law when both investor and respondent are EU Member States. Most Spain ECT claimants are EU-domiciled (Germany, Luxembourg, Netherlands), making their awards nearly unenforceable within the EU. Non-EU investors (Japan, Switzerland) are unaffected by this doctrine.

**Spain's non-compliance record:** As of the 2024 Report on Compliance with Investment Treaty Awards, Spain had 24 unpaid awards totalling at least USD 1.5 billion plus ~USD 350 million in interest and costs — the world's worst record among treaty signatories. Spain has refused to pay virtually all decided awards and systematically challenges enforcement in every available forum.

**The European Commission's state aid campaign:** The EC has intervened repeatedly, declaring that paying intra-EU ECT awards constitutes illegal state aid under TFEU Articles 107–108. On 24 March 2025 the Commission formally declared the Antin (Infrastructure Services) award illegal state aid and ordered Spain not to pay it. Similar proceedings are underway for other awards.

**Spain's ECT withdrawal:** Spain (and the EU as a whole) withdrew from the Energy Charter Treaty effective 28 June 2025. The ECT's sunset clause provides 20 years of continued investor protection for existing investments, so withdrawal does not extinguish pending or future claims on pre-withdrawal investments — but it signals a clear political direction.

**Key enforcement jurisdictions:**
- **EU courts (Germany, France, Belgium, Netherlands):** Systematically refusing enforcement of intra-EU ECT awards under Achmea/Komstroy.
- **Sweden:** Set aside Novenergia award (Dec 2022); Supreme Court confirmed (July 2023). Hostile to intra-EU awards.
- **Australia:** Highly creditor-friendly. Federal Court of Australia enforced multiple ICSID awards (Blasket/RREEF, 9REN, NextEra, Watkins — Aug 2025). Rejected sovereign immunity argument. Spain intends to appeal.
- **United States:** DC Circuit in *NextEra* (Aug 2024) confirmed jurisdiction to enforce ICSID awards, rejecting EU comity arguments. US DC District Court enforced JGC (Sept 2024) and is proceeding on others. SCOTUS had multiple petitions from Spain pending in Oct 2025.
- **Singapore:** Registration order for NextEra granted Jan 2024; Spain applied to set aside (May 2025).
- **United Kingdom:** Court of Appeal dismissed Spain's appeal in Antin (Oct 2024); UK Supreme Court granted Spain permission to appeal (Jan 2025). English High Court ruled ICSID/ECT awards are not assignable to third parties (Nov 2025), creating split with Australian courts.
- **Colombia:** Supreme Court refused to recognise PV Investors award.
- **Netherlands/Amsterdam:** District Court ruled (Feb 2025) that enforcement of PV Investors award constitutes state aid, blocking enforcement.

### Case No. 1318 — Maffezini v. Spain
**Filed:** 1997 | **Status:** Decided in favour of investor  
**Treaty:** Argentina–Spain BIT (1991)  
**Rules:** ICSID (ARB/97/7)  
**Home state:** Argentina  
**Sector:** Chemical products manufacturing (Galicia)  
**Claimed:** Not disclosed | **Awarded:** Not quantified in UNCTAD (amount was relatively modest, in the millions EUR)

**Background:** Emilio Agustín Maffezini, an Argentine national, invested in a company producing and distributing chemical products in the autonomous community of Galicia. He alleged that SODIGA (a Spanish state-owned regional development agency) improperly interfered in the management of his investment, forcing him to make investments he had not approved and causing significant financial losses.

**Legal basis:** Fair and equitable treatment and expropriation-related claims under the Argentina–Spain BIT.

**Outcome:** On 13 November 2000 the tribunal found Spain liable and ordered compensation. The exact amount awarded was not publicly stated in aggregate but is understood to be several million USD. Spain also sought rectification of the award (granted 31 January 2001, correcting a minor textual error).

**Significance:** This case is primarily famous not for its facts but for its landmark jurisdictional ruling (January 2000), in which the tribunal held that an MFN clause in the Argentina–Spain BIT could be used to import more favourable dispute-settlement provisions from a third-party BIT Spain had concluded. The "Maffezini doctrine" remains the most-cited (and most-contested) position in ISDS on MFN and dispute settlement.

**Enforcement:** As an early ICSID award from 2000 predating the intra-EU controversy, it is believed to have been settled or paid. Spain had not yet adopted its systematic non-compliance posture. No record of outstanding enforcement proceedings found.

**Enforcement status: LIKELY PAID / RESOLVED**

### Case No. 916 — AES Solar and others (The PV Investors) v. Spain
**Filed:** 2011 | **Status:** Decided in favour of investor  
**Treaty:** Energy Charter Treaty  
**Rules:** UNCITRAL (not ICSID)  
**Institution:** Permanent Court of Arbitration (PCA)  
**Home states:** Germany, Luxembourg, Netherlands, Austria and others (EU investors — fully intra-EU)  
**Sector:** Solar photovoltaic  
**Claimed:** EUR 1,900M (USD 2,200M) | **Awarded:** EUR ~91.1M (USD ~99.8M)

**Background:** A consortium of European solar energy investors challenged Spain's post-2012 RD reforms cutting photovoltaic feed-in tariffs and introducing the 7% generation tax. The group included AES Solar Energy, Ontario Power and numerous European institutional investors.

**Legal basis:** Fair and equitable treatment (Article 10(1) ECT) — frustrated legitimate expectations created by Spain's original FIT framework.

**Outcome:** Final Award issued 28 February 2020. Tribunal awarded approximately €91.1M (USD 99.8M), a fraction of the €1.9B claimed. The dramatic haircut (less than 5% of claimed amount) reflected the tribunal's view that Spain's legitimate expectation breach was more limited in scope than claimed.

**Enforcement:**
- **United States (DC Circuit, Aug 2024):** DC Circuit ruled that US courts have jurisdiction under FSIA. This was a key precedent-setting decision alongside NextEra.
- **Netherlands/Amsterdam (Feb 2025):** Amsterdam District Court held that enforcement constitutes illegal state aid under EU law.
- **Colombia:** Supreme Court refused recognition of the award.
- **Sweden:** Proceedings ongoing (this was a non-ICSID award so different procedural path from ICSID cases).
- Spain has not paid.

**Enforcement status: NOT PAID — actively contested in multiple forums; enforcement blocked in Netherlands; prospects dependent on non-EU jurisdictions**

### Case No. 821 — Charanne B.V. and Construction Investments S.à r.l. v. Spain
**Filed:** 2012 | **Status:** Decided in favour of State  
**Treaty:** Energy Charter Treaty  
**Rules:** SCC (Stockholm Chamber of Commerce)  
**Seat:** Madrid  
**Home states:** Netherlands (Charanne), Luxembourg (Construction Investments)  
**Sector:** Solar photovoltaic  
**Claimed:** EUR 17.8M (USD 20.7M) | **Awarded:** None

**Background:** The first of the major Spain renewables arbitration awards to be decided. Claimants had invested in Spanish solar PV plants relying on the FIT regime under Royal Decree 661/2007. Spain's early regulatory changes (pre-2013) reduced tariffs and imposed a moratorium.

**Legal basis:** Fair and equitable treatment and indirect expropriation under ECT Article 10(1).

**Outcome:** Final Award issued 21 January 2016. The tribunal (chaired by Alexis Mourre) dismissed all claims. It held that the early-phase regulatory changes (specifically the moratorium on new installations and minor modifications to existing tariff categories) did not violate the FET standard because investors could not have had a legitimate expectation that Spain's renewable energy regime would never change. This was the first major Spain ECT case to be decided and came out in Spain's favour — significantly influencing the legal debate about the threshold for legitimate expectations in regulatory contexts.

**Significance:** As the first Spain ECT award, this ruling set an early (and arguably narrow) interpretation of legitimate expectations that Spain repeatedly invoked in subsequent cases — with diminishing success as later tribunals distinguished or rejected it.

**Enforcement:** No award, so no enforcement issue.

**Enforcement status: N/A — Spain won**

**Outcome:** Tribunal found for Spain. Details of the merits decision are not fully public.

**Enforcement:** No award, so no enforcement issue.

### Case No. 763 — Eiser Infrastructure Limited and Energía Solar Luxembourg S.à r.l. v. Spain
**Filed:** 2013 | **Status:** Decided in favour of investor (original); ANNULLED; resubmission award issued Oct 2025  
**Treaty:** Energy Charter Treaty  
**Rules:** ICSID (ARB/13/36)  
**Home states:** United Kingdom (Eiser), Luxembourg (Energía Solar)  
**Sector:** Concentrated Solar Power (CSP)  
**Claimed:** EUR 256M (USD 279.5M) | **Original awarded:** EUR 128M (USD 139.8M) | **Resubmission award:** EUR 262M

**Background:** Eiser Infrastructure and Energía Solar invested in three CSP plants in Spain operated by Torresol Energy, a joint venture partly owned by Masdar. They invested in reliance on Spain's 2007–2008 CSP support framework, which was then systematically dismantled from 2012 onwards.

**Legal basis:** Fair and equitable treatment (FET) under ECT Article 10(1). Claimants argued Spain's changes amounted to a "radical" transformation of the regulatory regime on which they had relied.

**Outcome of original proceedings:** Award of EUR 128M issued in May 2017 — a major investor victory establishing that Spain had radically altered and abolished the special regime investors had reasonably relied upon, breaching FET.

**Annulment (June 2020):** ICSID ad hoc committee annulled the entire award on the grounds that arbitrator Stanimir Alexandrov had failed to disclose his consulting firm's ongoing relationship with Brattle Group, the claimants' expert witness firm. The committee found this constituted improper constitution of the tribunal AND a severe departure from a fundamental rule of procedure (one of the most drastic ICSID annulment outcomes in recent history).

**Resubmission (2021–2025):** Claimants resubmitted to a fresh ICSID tribunal. On 16 October 2025, the resubmission tribunal issued an award of EUR 262 million — more than double the original amount (reflecting higher interest accumulation over the longer period). Spain immediately filed for annulment of the resubmission award.

**Enforcement:** The resubmission award is subject to Spain's pending annulment application. The EC's state aid concerns (expressed regarding Antin and other awards) may also be invoked. UK and Australian enforcement proceedings likely to follow. As of early 2026, NOT PAID.

**Enforcement status: NOT PAID — new €262M award pending Spain's annulment application; EU state aid complications expected**

### Case No. 774 — Infrastructure Services Luxembourg S.à r.l. and Energia Termosolar B.V. (formerly Antin Infrastructure Services Luxembourg and Antin Energia Termosolar) v. Spain
**Filed:** 2013 | **Status:** Decided in favour of investor  
**Treaty:** Energy Charter Treaty  
**Rules:** ICSID (ARB/13/31)  
**Home states:** Luxembourg (ISL), Netherlands (Energia Termosolar)  
**Sector:** Concentrated Solar Power (CSP)  
**Claimed:** EUR 196M (USD 229.6M) | **Awarded:** EUR 101M (USD 115.3M)

**Background:** Antin Infrastructure Services (later renamed Infrastructure Services Luxembourg / ISL) held equity stakes in Spanish CSP plants and challenged Spain's post-2012 regulatory overhaul as a breach of its ECT FET obligations. The investment was made in reliance on Spain's original CSP support framework.

**Legal basis:** Fair and equitable treatment under ECT Article 10(1). The tribunal applied the standard established in Eiser and found Spain had radically and fundamentally changed the regulatory framework.

**Outcome:** Award issued October 2018 ordering Spain to pay EUR 101M plus interest.

**Enforcement (comprehensive):**
- **US (DC District Court, Jan 2025):** Court lifted the stay of enforcement proceedings (which had been pending the EU law arguments). The case is proceeding.
- **US Supreme Court (Oct 2025):** SCOTUS order issued — likely denying Spain's certiorari petition, meaning the DC Circuit's pro-enforcement ruling stands.
- **Australia:** Enforcement proceedings active.
- **UK:** Court of Appeal dismissed Spain's appeal (Oct 2024). UK Supreme Court granted Spain permission to appeal (Jan 2025) — proceedings ongoing.
- **Luxembourg:** Spain filed proceedings against Antin in Luxembourg courts to block enforcement.
- **European Commission (24 March 2025 — KEY DEVELOPMENT):** The Commission issued a formal state aid decision declaring that if Spain pays the €101M award it would constitute illegal and incompatible state aid under TFEU Articles 107 and 108(3). The Commission ordered Spain NOT to pay and to prevent any enforcement domestically or abroad. This is the first time the EC has formally declared an investment arbitration award to constitute state aid. Spain is using this decision as a shield in all enforcement jurisdictions.

**Enforcement status: NOT PAID — EC state aid decision is the most significant legal obstacle; enforcement blocked in EU; actively litigated in UK and US**

### Case No. 794 — RREEF Infrastructure (G.P.) Limited and RREEF Pan-European Infrastructure Two Lux S.à r.l. v. Spain
**Filed:** 2013 | **Status:** Decided in favour of investor  
**Treaty:** Energy Charter Treaty  
**Rules:** ICSID (ARB/13/30)  
**Home states:** United Kingdom (RREEF GP), Luxembourg (RREEF Pan-European)  
**Sector:** Wind and solar energy  
**Claimed:** EUR 441M (USD 512.3M) | **Awarded:** EUR 59.6M (USD 66M)

**Background:** RREEF Infrastructure (part of Deutsche Asset Management / DWS) invested in wind and solar energy assets in Spain. The claim challenged Spain's comprehensive dismantling of the renewable energy incentive framework between 2012 and 2014.

**Legal basis:** Fair and equitable treatment and expropriation under ECT.

**Outcome:** Award of EUR 59.6M issued 11 December 2019.

**Enforcement:**

- **UK (English High Court, Nov 2025):** The High Court ruled that ICSID and ECT awards are NOT assignable to third parties (contra Australia). This ruling blocked Blasket's UK enforcement of the RREEF award, creating a significant split between Australian and English courts on assignability.
- Spain has not paid.

**Enforcement status: NOT PAID — award enforced by Australian court (appeal pending); UK enforcement blocked on assignability grounds; EU courts refused**

### Case No. 722 — NextEra Energy Global Holdings B.V. and NextEra Energy Spain Holdings B.V. v. Kingdom of Spain
**Filed:** 2014 | **Status:** Decided in favour of investor  
**Treaty:** Energy Charter Treaty  
**Rules:** ICSID (ARB/14/11)  
**Home states:** Netherlands (both entities are Dutch subsidiaries of US energy giant NextEra Energy Inc.)  
**Sector:** Wind and solar energy  
**Claimed:** EUR 521.4M (USD 586M) | **Awarded:** EUR 290.6M (USD 323.6M)

**Background:** NextEra Energy (Florida-based US energy company, the world's largest generator of wind and solar power) held Spanish renewable energy assets through Dutch subsidiaries. The claim challenged Spain's comprehensive elimination of the original renewable energy support framework.

**Legal basis:** Fair and equitable treatment under ECT Article 10(1). The award found Spain had radically transformed the regulatory environment for renewable energy.

**Outcome:** Award of EUR 290.6M issued 31 May 2019. This is the largest decided award in the Spain ECT wave. A revision award was also issued (2024), after Spain's revision request was rejected.

**Enforcement (most litigated case in the wave):**
- **ICSID Revision (Jan 2024):** ICSID tribunal rejected Spain's revision request as manifestly without legal merit; enforcement stay automatically terminated.
- **US DC Circuit (Aug 2024):** Landmark ruling confirming US courts have jurisdiction under FSIA to enforce ICSID awards, rejecting Spain's EU comity/Achmea arguments. This is now the leading US precedent.
- **US Supreme Court (Oct 2025):** SCOTUS order issued re Spain's certiorari petition. Based on context, likely denying cert (allowing DC Circuit ruling to stand) or the petition was disposed of.
- **Australia (Aug 2025):** Federal Court of Australia enforced the award as part of the Blasket judgment [2025] FCA 1028. Spain intends to appeal.
- **Singapore (Jan 2024 / May 2025):** Registration order granted January 2024. Spain applied to set aside (May 2025) on sovereign immunity grounds.
- Spain has not paid.

**Enforcement status: NOT PAID — award enforced in Australia (appeal pending); DC Circuit confirmed US jurisdiction; enforcement actively pursued in Singapore and elsewhere; most significant test case for ICSID enforcement outside EU**

### Case No. 656 — Novenergia II - Energy & Environment (SCA) (Grand Duchy of Luxembourg), SICAR v. Kingdom of Spain
**Filed:** 2015 | **Status:** Decided in favour of investor (then AWARD SET ASIDE in Sweden)  
**Treaty:** Energy Charter Treaty  
**Rules:** SCC (Stockholm Chamber of Commerce, Case No. 2015/063)  
**Home state:** Luxembourg  
**Sector:** Solar photovoltaic  
**Claimed:** EUR 61.3M (USD 75.9M) | **Awarded:** EUR 53.3M (USD 66M) — then set aside

**Background:** Novenergia II purchased eight solar parks in Spain in 2007–2008, relying on the FIT framework. Between 2010–2014, Spain's regulatory changes drastically reduced the remuneration these plants received.

**Legal basis:** Fair and equitable treatment under ECT Article 10(1).

**Outcome:** Original award of EUR 53.3M (February 2018).

**Swedish set-aside proceedings:**
- Spain challenged the award before Swedish courts (seat of arbitration was Stockholm).
- **Svea Court of Appeal (December 2022):** Set aside the award entirely, applying Achmea and Komstroy to hold that the SCC arbitration clause in the ECT is incompatible with EU law as applied between two EU Member States. This was one of the first major judicial set-asides of an ECT award on Achmea/Komstroy grounds.
- **Swedish Supreme Court (July 2023):** Confirmed the Svea Court's ruling, definitively setting aside the Novenergia award under Swedish law.

**Significance:** Novenergia is the landmark case for the intra-EU ECT enforcement problem in Sweden and broader Europe. The award is effectively nullified from a Swedish-law perspective. Enforcement in other jurisdictions may still be pursued but the set-aside at the seat is a major blow.

**Enforcement status: AWARD SET ASIDE by Swedish courts (Dec 2022, confirmed July 2023) — effectively unenforceable in most jurisdictions; Spain has not paid and the award is legally nullified at the seat**

## Key Enforcement Facts (as of mid-2026)

1. **Spain is the world's most non-compliant state** with investment treaty awards (2024 Report): 24 unpaid awards, USD 1.5B+ outstanding.
2. **EU courts have uniformly refused** enforcement of intra-EU ECT awards under Achmea/Komstroy.
3. **Australian Federal Court** enforced 4 ICSID awards worth ~€470M in August 2025 (Blasket: RREEF, 9REN, NextEra, Watkins). Spain intends to appeal.
4. **US courts** are enforcing — DC Circuit confirmed jurisdiction for ICSID awards (NextEra, Aug 2024); DC District Court enforced JGC award (Sept 2024).
5. **European Commission** declared Antin award payment illegal state aid (March 2025) — weapon Spain uses against enforcement everywhere.
6. **English High Court** ruled ICSID/ECT awards are not assignable to third parties (Blasket/OperaFund, Nov 2025) — creating a UK/Australia split.
7. **Sweden set aside** the Novenergia award (2022/2023) — definitively nullified at the seat.
8. **Japan/Switzerland investors** (JGC, Eurus Holdings, Itochu, EBL) face NO intra-EU obstacles. JGC enforced in US (2024). These are the "clean" awards.
9. **Eiser resubmission** produced €262M award (Oct 2025), more than double the annulled original. Spain filing for annulment.
10. **Spain–ECT withdrawal** effective June 2025 — sunset clause applies to existing investments for 20 years.
